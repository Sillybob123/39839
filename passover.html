Passover: From Narrowness to Freedom

Passover (Pesach) tells the story of the Exodus from Egypt: a journey from oppression to dignity, from fear to trust, and from spiritual constriction to open possibility. Every generation is invited to experience that freedom personally.

The Story of Passover in Brief

The Israelites became enslaved in Egypt under Pharaoh. Their labor was harsh, and their future seemed sealed. G-d then chose Moses to lead them out, sending signs and plagues that revealed Divine justice and power.

On the final night, Jewish families marked their doors and prepared to leave quickly. They baked unleavened bread, matzah, because there was no time for dough to rise. At dawn, freedom began.

The Exodus is not only history. It is a spiritual template: we each leave our own "Egypt" whenever we break free from habits, fears, and limitations that keep us from serving G-d with clarity and joy.

The Hebrew name "Mitzrayim" (Egypt) can also suggest "narrow places." Passover is the work of expanding the soul.

Passover Essentials

  • Remove chametz (leaven) from the home.
  • Eat matzah and avoid chametz during the holiday.
  • Tell the Exodus story at the Seder table.
  • Ask questions, sing, and involve everyone.

Central idea: freedom is not just "freedom from" slavery, but "freedom for" purpose, mitzvot, and holiness.

The Seder: A Night of Memory and Awakening

The Seder is designed as an experience, not just a reading. We drink four cups, eat symbolic foods, retell the story, and move from brokenness to praise. The order guides us to feel that we personally went out of Egypt.

  • Matzah: humility, simplicity, and faith.
  • Maror (bitter herbs): the bitterness of slavery and suffering.
  • Charoset: a reminder of mortar and labor, sweetened by hope.
  • Four cups of wine: four expressions of redemption.

A powerful Seder includes explanation, warmth, singing, and thoughtful conversation. Children asking "Why is this night different?" is not a side detail; it is the heart of transmission.

Preparing with Intention

  1. One to two weeks before: begin home preparation gradually and review Seder plans.
  2. Days before: gather matzah, wine/grape juice, Haggadot, and Seder plate items.
  3. Erev Pesach: finish final chametz removal and shift into holiday calm.
  4. During Pesach: slow down, tell the story, and choose one personal growth area as your own Exodus.

Passover preparation can feel intense, but approached with purpose it becomes deeply meaningful: clearing physical space to open spiritual space.

A Reflection for Your Table

Ask: What is one "narrow place" I am ready to leave this year? Name it. Pray over it. Share it with someone you trust. Let your Seder become not only a memory of ancient redemption, but the beginning of your next chapter.

May your Passover be filled with clarity, courage, and joy, and may your home be blessed with meaningful conversation, song, and deep gratitude.
